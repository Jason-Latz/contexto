The quiet power of reading

Notes on how we actually learn a language

Every morning, before the city wakes, a student sits by the window with a book and a cup of coffee. She reads one article about science, another about history, and a short story about a family in a small town. Each page brings a new word she has never seen, but the sentence around it carries enough meaning for her to continue without stopping.

Her teacher at the university often says that a language is not learned in a single class. It grows slowly, through practice, the way a child learns to speak at home. A person meets the same word in a dozen different places — in a song, on a street sign, in a question from a friend — until its meaning feels almost obvious, and no answer key is ever required.

By the end of the year, the student notices something strange. The book that once felt difficult now feels easy. She did not memorize a list or finish a course. She simply read, a little every day, and let the context do the teaching. That, she thinks, is the quiet power of reading — a school without walls, open on every page.
